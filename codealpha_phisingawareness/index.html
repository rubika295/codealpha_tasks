Recognizing Phishing Emails

Learn to identify the red flags in suspicious emails

Urgent Language

"Act now!", "Your account will be suspended", "Immediate action required"

Example: "URGENT: Verify your account within 24 hours or it will be permanently deleted!"

Suspicious Links

Hover over links to check the actual URL before clicking

Bad: http://security-update.com/login
Good: https://paypal.com/login

Sender Mismatch

Check the sender's email address carefully

Suspicious: support@apple-icloud.com
Legitimate: support@apple.com

Poor Grammar

Spelling mistakes and grammatical errors are common red flags

Example: "We has detected suspicios activity on your acount. Please click here to secure."

Unexpected Offers

Prizes, inheritances, or job offers you weren't expecting

Example: "Congratulations! You've won $1,000,000 in our lottery!"

Attachments

Unexpected attachments, especially .exe, .zip, or .scr files

Dangerous: invoice_93847.exe
Suspicious: document.zip

Real Phishing Email Example

From: security@netflix-security.com
Subject: URGENT: Account Suspension Notice

Dear Netflix Member,

We have detected unusual activity on your account. Your subscription will be suspended within 24 hours unless you verify your payment information.

Click here to verify your account

If you don't take action immediately, you will lose access to your account and all saved content.

Netflix Security Team

Red Flags in this email:

  • Urgent language and threats
  • Suspicious sender domain (netflix-security.com instead of netflix.com)
  • Generic greeting ("Dear Netflix Member")
  • Link without clear destination

Identifying Fake Websites

Learn to spot counterfeit websites designed to steal your information

Check the URL

  • Look for HTTPS (padlock icon)
  • Verify the exact domain spelling
  • Watch for subdomains like "secure.bank.com" vs "bank.com"

SSL Certificate

  • Click the padlock to view certificate details
  • Verify it's issued to the correct company
  • Check if the certificate is valid

Website Design

  • Poor quality images or layout
  • Broken links or missing pages
  • Outdated design or branding

Language and Content

  • Grammar and spelling errors
  • Inconsistent terminology
  • Poor translation quality

Social Engineering Tactics

Understanding the psychological tricks attackers use

Impersonation

Attackers pretend to be someone you trust - your boss, IT department, or a known company.

Example: "Hi, this is John from IT. I need your password to update the system."

Emotional Manipulation

Using fear, curiosity, or urgency to override rational thinking.

Example: "Your computer has a virus! Click here immediately to remove it!"

Authority

Claiming to be from a position of power to demand compliance.

Example: "This is the IRS. You owe back taxes and must pay immediately."

Social Proof

Creating the illusion that many others are taking the same action.

Example: "Join 10,000+ people who have already secured their accounts!"

Reciprocity

Offering something small to make you feel obligated to give something back.

Example: "Free gift card! Just enter your details to claim."

Scarcity

Creating artificial scarcity to prompt immediate action.

Example: "Only 3 spots left! Limited time offer - expires in 1 hour!"

Why Social Engineering Works

Cognitive Biases

Our brains take mental shortcuts that attackers exploit

Time Pressure

Urgent situations reduce our ability to think critically

Trust Assumption

We tend to trust people and institutions by default

Risk Misjudgment

We underestimate the likelihood of becoming victims

Best Practices & Safety Tips

Essential habits to protect yourself from phishing attacks

Email Safety

  • Always verify sender addresses before clicking links
  • Hover over links to preview the actual URL
  • Be suspicious of emails with urgent or threatening language
  • Never provide sensitive information via email
  • Use separate email accounts for different purposes

Password Security

  • Use unique, complex passwords for each account
  • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) everywhere possible
  • Use a password manager to store credentials securely
  • Change passwords immediately after suspected breaches
  • Avoid using personal information in passwords

Website Safety

  • Always check for HTTPS and the padlock icon
  • Bookmark important websites to avoid typos
  • Verify website URLs before entering credentials
  • Be cautious of pop-ups and redirects
  • Use browser security extensions and updates

Mobile Security

  • Be extra careful with small screens - URLs are harder to see
  • Install apps only from official app stores
  • Keep your operating system and apps updated
  • Use mobile security software
  • Avoid public Wi-Fi for sensitive transactions

What to Do If You Suspect Phishing

1

Stop & Think

Take a moment to evaluate the situation rationally

2

Verify Independently

Contact the supposed sender through official channels

3

Report It

Report phishing attempts to your IT department or relevant authorities

4

Delete & Block

Remove the message and block the sender

5

Change Passwords

If you clicked anything, change affected passwords immediately
